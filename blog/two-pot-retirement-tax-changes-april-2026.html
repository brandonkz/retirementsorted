Two-Pot Retirement Tax Changes April 2026 — What You Need to Know

Published 30 March 2026 · 7 min read

April 2026 brings the start of a new tax year and a few changes that affect your two-pot retirement savings. Nothing dramatic has changed in the two-pot system itself — it still works the same way it has since September 2024. But the tax brackets, thresholds, and VAT adjustments that come with the new tax year will impact how much you actually get to keep when you withdraw from your savings pot.

Let's break it down in plain language.

Quick Two-Pot Refresher

Since September 2024, all South African retirement fund contributions are split into three components:

  • Savings pot (1/3): You can withdraw from this once per tax year, minimum R2,000. It's taxed as income.
  • Retirement pot (2/3): Locked until you retire. Can't touch it.
  • Vested pot: Your pre-September 2024 balance. Old rules still apply.

The big draw (pun intended) is the savings pot. It gives you access to some of your retirement money before you turn 55 — useful for emergencies, debt, or life-happens moments. But SARS takes their cut, and that's where most people get tripped up.

How Savings Pot Withdrawals Are Taxed

This is the critical thing most articles get wrong, so let's be precise:

Savings pot withdrawals are taxed at your marginal income tax rate. SARS treats the withdrawal as additional income on top of your salary. If you earn R350,000 per year, you're in the 26% tax bracket. A R30,000 savings pot withdrawal gets taxed at that same 26% — so you'd receive roughly R22,200 after tax.

Your fund administrator deducts the tax before paying you. The amount shown in your fund statement is pre-tax. What lands in your bank account is less.

⚠️ Common mistake: Some people think savings pot withdrawals are taxed at a flat rate. They're not. The tax depends entirely on your total annual income. Higher earners pay more tax on the same withdrawal amount.

💡 Key takeaway: Someone earning R900k/year loses nearly 40% of their withdrawal to tax. Someone earning R100k keeps over 80%. The system effectively penalises higher earners more for accessing their savings pot.

April 2026 VAT & Threshold Changes

From 1 April 2026, SARS has adjusted VAT registration thresholds. This mostly affects small business owners and freelancers, but here's why it matters for retirement:

  • If you're self-employed and withdrawing from your savings pot, the withdrawal is still taxed as income — but your total taxable income (business profit + withdrawal) determines your bracket.
  • The VAT threshold adjustment means some sole proprietors who were close to the registration boundary may now fall below it, simplifying their tax obligations.
  • If you're a freelancer earning in USD and also making a two-pot withdrawal, be especially careful about which tax bracket you land in. The combined income could bump you up a bracket.

Strategies to Minimise Tax on Your Savings Pot

1. Time Your Withdrawal Strategically

If you can choose when to withdraw, do it in a tax year where your income is lower. Starting a new job? Took a few months off? Went part-time? That's the year to make your savings pot withdrawal — your marginal rate will be lower.

2. Don't Withdraw More Than You Need

Every rand you withdraw is taxed. If you need R15,000 for a car repair, withdraw R15,000 — not R30,000 "while you're at it." The extra R15,000 you don't need is still being taxed, and that money was growing tax-free inside your fund.

3. Max Out Your Deductions First

Before withdrawing, make sure you're claiming all tax deductions you're entitled to:

  • Retirement fund contributions (up to 27.5% of income, capped at R350,000/year)
  • Medical aid tax credits
  • Travel allowance deductions (if applicable)
  • Home office expenses (if you qualify)

More deductions = lower taxable income = lower marginal rate on your withdrawal.

4. Consider the Opportunity Cost

Scenario Withdraw R25,000 Now Leave It Invested (10yr)
After tax (31% bracket) R17,250 in your pocket —
Value at retirement (8% growth) R17,250 (if saved elsewhere) ~R54,000
Difference Leaving it invested is worth ~R37,000 more over 10 years

That R25,000 withdrawal costs you a lot more than just the tax. You're also losing compound growth. Only withdraw if you genuinely need the money now.

Using the SARS Two-Pot Calculator

SARS has an official savings pot calculator on their website. Here's how to use it:

  1. Enter your annual taxable income (before the withdrawal)
  2. Enter the amount you plan to withdraw from your savings pot
  3. The calculator shows your estimated tax and net payout

It's straightforward, but remember: the calculator gives an estimate based on current tax tables. If you're near a bracket boundary, the actual amount may differ slightly. For precise numbers, speak to a tax practitioner.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is a two-pot savings pot withdrawal taxed?

It's added to your taxable income for the year and taxed at your marginal rate. If you earn R400,000/year (31% bracket) and withdraw R25,000, that R25,000 is taxed at 31%. SARS deducts the tax before the money reaches you.

Can I make more than one withdrawal per tax year?

Yes. Each withdrawal must be at least R2,000. There's no cap on the number of withdrawals, but each is taxed and your fund may charge per-withdrawal fees. Strategically, fewer larger withdrawals are usually cheaper than many small ones.

What changed for retirement in April 2026?

The two-pot system itself hasn't changed structurally. The main changes are VAT threshold adjustments and the new tax year's bracket parameters. These can affect your effective rate on withdrawals, especially if you're self-employed or near a bracket boundary.

Should I withdraw from my savings pot to pay off debt?

It depends on the interest rate. If you're paying 20%+ on a credit card, withdrawing to clear it might make mathematical sense (after accounting for tax). If it's a home loan at 11%, the compound growth inside your fund probably beats it. Do the maths for your specific situation.

Can SARS take my savings pot withdrawal if I owe tax?

Yes. Your fund administrator is legally required to comply with any SARS directive to withhold funds for outstanding tax debts. Sort your tax affairs before requesting a withdrawal.
