Preservation Fund vs Retirement Annuity South Africa 2026

Published 30 March 2026 · 7 min read

You've left a job — retrenched, resigned, or changed companies. Your pension or provident fund balance is sitting there, and your HR department is asking what you want to do with it. The two most common options: transfer to a preservation fund, or transfer to a retirement annuity (RA).

They sound similar. They're both retirement vehicles. But they have important differences, especially under the two-pot system. Here's the honest breakdown.

What's a Preservation Fund? What's an RA?

Preservation Fund

A preservation fund is specifically designed to receive money from an employer retirement fund (pension or provident fund) when you leave a job. You can't make new contributions — it only holds what you transfer in. The big feature: you get one withdrawal before retirement age from the vested component (your pre-September 2024 balance).

Retirement Annuity (RA)

An RA is a personal retirement savings vehicle. You can contribute to it voluntarily — monthly debit orders, annual lump sums, whatever suits you. There's no early withdrawal from the vested component until you turn 55 (or in very specific cases like emigration or disability). You can also transfer pension/provident fund money into an RA, but you'd lose that one-withdrawal benefit.

How the Two-Pot System Changed Things

Before September 2024, the main difference was clear: preservation funds gave you one withdrawal, RAs gave you zero. That made preservation funds strictly better for anyone who wanted a safety net.

Under two-pot, both now have a savings pot you can access. So the gap has narrowed. But there's a crucial nuance:

💡 Key distinction: Your vested component (pre-September 2024 money) still follows the old rules. In a preservation fund, you still get your one vested withdrawal. In an RA, that vested money is locked until 55. The two-pot savings pot is in addition to the vested withdrawal — they're separate pots with separate rules.

So if you transfer to a preservation fund, you get:

  • Savings pot access (from post-September 2024 growth)
  • PLUS one vested withdrawal from the old balance

If you transfer to an RA, you get:

  • Savings pot access (from contributions and growth)
  • No vested withdrawal option
  • BUT tax deductions on ongoing contributions

Which Is Better After Retrenchment?

Retrenchment is stressful enough without having to make complex financial decisions under pressure. Here's the straightforward answer:

Transfer to a preservation fund first. Here's why:

  1. You keep your one vested withdrawal. If you need emergency cash while job hunting, you can access a portion of your vested balance. This is your financial safety net.
  2. You can always transfer to an RA later. Moving from preservation fund → RA is easy and tax-free. Moving from RA → preservation fund is not possible.
  3. It's a holding pattern, not a permanent decision. Park the money, take a breath, and make the RA decision later when you're employed again and thinking clearly.

Tax Benefits Compared

Contributions

Only RAs give you a tax deduction on contributions. You can deduct up to 27.5% of your taxable income (or remuneration, whichever is greater), capped at R350,000 per year. This includes employer fund contributions, so don't double-count.

Preservation funds don't accept new contributions, so there's no deduction to claim.

Growth Inside the Fund

Both are identical: investment growth inside the fund is tax-free. No capital gains tax, no dividend withholding tax. This is one of the biggest advantages of both vehicles — your money compounds without SARS taking a cut along the way.

At Withdrawal / Retirement

Both follow the same tax treatment:

  • Savings pot withdrawals: Taxed at marginal rate (same for both)
  • Vested withdrawal: Taxed per the retirement lump sum tax table (preservation fund only)
  • At retirement: First R550,000 as a lump sum is tax-free, rest per the retirement tax table

When to Use Each — Decision Framework

Use a Preservation Fund When:

  • You're leaving an employer and want to keep your vested withdrawal option
  • You're between jobs and need a financial safety net
  • You're not sure what to do yet (preservation fund = good default)
  • You want to keep old retirement money separate from new savings

Use a Retirement Annuity When:

  • You're self-employed and need a retirement savings vehicle with tax benefits
  • You want to make ongoing voluntary contributions
  • You've already used your one preservation fund withdrawal and want your remaining money locked away
  • You're disciplined enough not to need the withdrawal option
  • You want to reduce your taxable income (high earners benefit most)
⚠️ Don't cash out: The worst option is cashing out your pension when you leave a job. You'll pay tax on the full amount, plus you lose decades of compound growth. A R200,000 pension cashed out at 30 could have been worth R2M+ at retirement. Transfer, don't cash out.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a preservation fund and a retirement annuity?

A preservation fund holds money transferred from a previous employer's pension or provident fund. You get one pre-retirement withdrawal from the vested component. A retirement annuity is a personal savings vehicle you contribute to voluntarily — no vested withdrawal, but you get tax deductions on contributions.

Can I withdraw from a preservation fund under the two-pot system?

Yes. You now have a savings pot component (from post-September 2024 contributions/growth) that allows withdrawals of minimum R2,000 per tax year. You also keep your one vested withdrawal from the pre-September 2024 balance. These are two separate withdrawal rights.

Which is better after retrenchment?

Preservation fund, almost always. You keep the one vested withdrawal as an emergency backstop while job hunting. You can always transfer to an RA later. The reverse (RA to preservation fund) isn't possible.

Can I have both a preservation fund and an RA?

Yes. Many South Africans have both — a preservation fund holding old employer money, and an RA for ongoing voluntary contributions. There's no rule against it, and it's often the smartest structure.

Is a preservation fund protected from creditors?

Yes. Both preservation funds and RAs are protected from creditors under the Pension Funds Act. If you're declared insolvent, your retirement savings in these vehicles can't be seized. This is another reason not to cash out.
