Two-Pot Retirement System South Africa Explained (2026 Update)

Last updated: March 23, 2026

If you have a retirement annuity, pension fund, or provident fund in South Africa, you're now part of the two-pot retirement system.

This system launched on 1 September 2024 and fundamentally changed how retirement savings work in SA. For the first time, you can access part of your retirement fund before you retire — but there's a catch.

Most South Africans don't fully understand how the two pots work, when you can withdraw, and what the tax consequences are. This guide breaks it down in plain English.

What Is the Two-Pot Retirement System?

The two-pot system splits your retirement fund into two separate pots:

1. Savings Pot (1/3 of contributions) 2. Retirement Pot (2/3 of contributions)

Every time you or your employer contributes to your retirement fund, the money is automatically split:

- 33.3% goes into the Savings Pot (accessible before retirement) - 66.7% goes into the Retirement Pot (locked until retirement)

Why Did the Government Introduce This?

The two-pot system is designed to balance emergency access with long-term retirement protection.

Before September 2024, South Africans could only access retirement funds when:

- Changing jobs (provident funds only) - Retiring (age 55+) - Emigrating permanently

This created a problem: people in financial distress had no legal way to access their retirement savings. Many resigned from jobs just to access provident funds, losing employer benefits and disrupting their careers.

The two-pot system gives you limited, controlled access to part of your retirement savings while protecting the majority for your actual retirement.

How the Two-Pot System Works

1. The Savings Pot (1/3 of Contributions)

What it is:

- One-third of every future contribution goes into the Savings Pot - You can withdraw from this pot once per tax year - Minimum withdrawal: R2,000 - Maximum withdrawal: 10% of the Savings Pot balance (removed in 2025 — now unlimited withdrawals up to full balance)

Key change (2026): The initial 10% cap was removed in early 2025. You can now withdraw the full Savings Pot balance once per tax year, as long as it's above the R2,000 minimum.

Tax treatment:

- Withdrawals are taxed as income at your marginal tax rate - No tax-free threshold (unlike retirement lump sums)

Example:

You earn R50,000/month. Your RA contribution is R5,000/month.

- R1,667 goes into the Savings Pot (33.3%) - R3,333 goes into the Retirement Pot (66.7%)

After 12 months, your Savings Pot has R20,000.

You withdraw R10,000. SARS taxes it at your marginal rate (31% for R50k/month earners) = R3,100 tax.

You receive R6,900 after tax.

---

2. The Retirement Pot (2/3 of Contributions)

What it is:

- Two-thirds of every future contribution goes into the Retirement Pot - Locked until you retire (age 55+) or leave your job - Cannot be accessed for emergencies - Designed to protect long-term retirement income

Tax treatment:

- Withdrawals at retirement are taxed according to the retirement lump sum tax table - First R550,000 is tax-free (2026 threshold) - Above R550,000: progressive tax rates (18%-36%)

When can you access it?

- Retirement (age 55+) - Resignation (only for provident funds — but you lose growth and compound interest) - Emigration (formal emigration via SARB) - Death (paid to beneficiaries)

---

3. The Vested Pot (Pre-1 September 2024 Savings)

Anything saved before 1 September 2024 sits in a separate "vested pot" and follows the old rules:

- Pension funds / RAs: Locked until retirement (no early access) - Provident funds: Accessible when you resign (but taxed heavily)

Key point: The vested pot does NOT convert into the savings or retirement pot. It remains separate and untouchable until retirement (or resignation for provident funds).

Should You Withdraw from Your Savings Pot?

This is the R10,000 question (literally).

The government estimates 1.2 million South Africans withdrew from their Savings Pots in the first six months. Total withdrawals exceeded R21 billion.

But should you withdraw?

When Withdrawal Makes Sense

✅ Medical emergencies (surgery, chronic medication, hospital bills) ✅ Debt trap (paying off high-interest debt like credit cards at 25%+) ✅ Avoiding home repossession (catching up on bond payments) ✅ Job loss (short-term survival while finding work) ✅ Education costs (your kids' school fees, not lifestyle)

When You Should NOT Withdraw

❌ Holiday / new car / lifestyle purchases (you'll regret it at 65) ❌ Paying off low-interest debt (bond at 11% vs retirement growth at 12%+) ❌ "Just because I can" (opportunity cost is massive) ❌ Impulse spending (R10k withdrawn today = R50k+ lost at retirement)

---

The Real Cost of Withdrawing

Let's say you're 35 years old. You withdraw R10,000 from your Savings Pot.

Scenario 1: You Withdraw and Spend It

- R10,000 today (taxed as income — you get ~R7,000 after tax) - Lost growth over 30 years (assuming 10% annual return): - R10,000 → R174,494 by age 65

What you actually gave up: R174,494 in retirement savings for R7,000 today.

Scenario 2: You Leave It Invested

- R10,000 stays invested for 30 years - Grows to R174,494 by age 65 - Provides R12,000+/year in retirement income (7% withdrawal rate)

Bottom line: Every R10,000 you withdraw today costs you R174,000+ in retirement wealth.

How Much Can You Actually Withdraw?

Pre-2025 Rules (10% Cap)

Originally, you could only withdraw 10% of your Savings Pot balance per year.

Example:

- Savings Pot balance: R50,000 - Maximum withdrawal: R5,000 (10%) - Tax (at 31% marginal rate): R1,550 - Net after tax: R3,450

Post-2025 Rules (No Cap — Full Balance)

From early 2025 onwards, the 10% cap was removed. You can now withdraw the full balance of your Savings Pot once per tax year (minimum R2,000).

Example:

- Savings Pot balance: R50,000 - Maximum withdrawal: R50,000 (full balance) - Tax (at 31% marginal rate): R15,500 - Net after tax: R34,500

Warning: Withdrawing your full Savings Pot resets your emergency buffer to zero. Future contributions take time to rebuild.

Tax on Savings Pot Withdrawals

Savings Pot withdrawals are taxed as income at your marginal tax rate.

2026/2027 Income Tax Rates (South Africa)

| Annual Income | Marginal Tax Rate | |---------------|-------------------| | R0 – R237,100 | 18% | | R237,101 – R370,500 | 26% | | R370,501 – R512,800 | 31% | | R512,801 – R673,000 | 36% | | R673,001 – R857,900 | 39% | | R857,901 and above | 45% |

Example:

You earn R600,000/year (R50,000/month). Your marginal tax rate is 36%.

You withdraw R20,000 from your Savings Pot.

- Tax: R7,200 (36% of R20,000) - Net received: R12,800

Key insight: The higher your income, the more tax you pay on withdrawals. High earners lose 36-45% to tax.

How to Withdraw from Your Savings Pot

The process varies by fund administrator, but generally:

Step 1: Contact Your Fund Administrator

- Retirement annuity: Contact your RA provider (e.g., Old Mutual, Sanlam, Allan Gray) - Pension / provident fund: Contact your employer's HR or fund administrator

Step 2: Submit Withdrawal Request

Most funds now have online portals or apps where you can request withdrawals.

You'll need:

- ID number - Bank account details (must match your ID) - Tax number

Step 3: Wait for Processing

- Processing time: 5-15 business days (varies by fund) - Tax deducted automatically (PAYE — your fund deducts tax before paying you)

Step 4: Receive Funds

Money is paid directly into your bank account. No cheques. No cash.

Common Mistakes South Africans Make with the Two-Pot System

Mistake 1: Withdrawing Every Year "Because I Can"

Just because you can withdraw doesn't mean you should.

Every withdrawal permanently reduces your retirement income. Compound interest works both ways — it builds wealth slowly, and it destroys wealth fast.

Mistake 2: Not Understanding the Tax Hit

Many South Africans see "R10,000 available" and assume they'll get R10,000.

If you're in the 31% tax bracket, you're actually getting R6,900. The rest goes to SARS.

Mistake 3: Thinking the Vested Pot Is Accessible

Your pre-September 2024 savings are NOT part of the Savings Pot. They follow the old rules (locked until retirement).

Mistake 4: Forgetting About Opportunity Cost

R10,000 withdrawn at age 35 = R174,000 lost at age 65 (10% growth over 30 years).

That R10,000 today better be solving a real emergency — not funding a holiday.

FAQs: Two-Pot Retirement System

Can I withdraw from both pots?

No. Only the Savings Pot is accessible before retirement. The Retirement Pot is locked until age 55+.

How often can I withdraw?

Once per tax year (March 1 – February 28).

What's the minimum withdrawal?

R2,000. If your Savings Pot balance is below R2,000, you can't withdraw.

Can I transfer money from the Retirement Pot to the Savings Pot?

No. The pots are separate and cannot be transferred between each other.

What happens to my Savings Pot if I change jobs?

It stays with your fund. If you move to a new employer, your new contributions split into the same two pots.

Can I opt out of the two-pot system?

No. All South Africans with retirement funds are automatically enrolled.

---

Final Thoughts: Is the Two-Pot System Good or Bad?

It depends on how you use it.

The Good

- Emergency access: No need to resign to access retirement funds - Financial flexibility: Helps people in genuine distress - Controlled access: Protects 2/3 of your retirement savings

The Bad

- Temptation to withdraw: Many people treat it like a savings account - Opportunity cost: Every withdrawal permanently reduces retirement income - Tax hit: Withdrawals are taxed as income (no tax-free threshold)

Bottom line: The two-pot system is a useful safety valve for emergencies. But if you treat it like an ATM, you'll regret it when you turn 65 and realize you've got R300,000 instead of R2 million.
